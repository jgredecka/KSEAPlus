Algorithm Overview



KARP

$$ K = { \sum^m_{i=1} \alpha_i \over \sum^n_{j=1} \beta_j } \cdot { \sqrt {m \over t} } \cdot {10^6} $$

  • KARP is decribed as the sum of intensities of a substrate group ($ \sum \alpha_i $) relative to that of all phosphosites (n) in the dataset ($ \sum \beta_j $).
  • The K score is normalised in terms of the total number of sites from the dataset found for a kinase (m) and total number of sites in the database for that kinase (t).
  • It is an absolute measure of activity; KARP does not rely on comparison of values between seperate groups, as seen above.
  • High K-scores indicate an upregulated kinase activity.

Z-Test

$$Z = {(\bar s - \bar p) \sqrt m \over \delta}$$

  • Z-Test is a parametric test; it is assumed the resulting scores are normally distributed.
  • It calculates the difference between the mean log2(FC) of a substrate group ($\bar s$) and the mean log2(FC) of all substrates in the dataset ($ \bar p$).
  • The Z score is normalised in terms of the number of phosphosites from the dataset found for a given kinase ($\sqrt m$) and the standard deviation of all phosphosite log2(FC) values ($\delta$).
  • For a FC = treatment/control, a positive Z-Score denotes a kinase with an upregulated activity. The opposite is true for negative scores.

Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS)


  • KS test is a non-parametric statistical test.
  • It examines phosphorylation differences between substrate and non-substrate log2(FC) distributions of a given kinase.
  • The test calculates the maximum difference (vertical distance) in cumulative fraction between the two distribution groups.
  • This difference is denoted as the KS statistic and is reported alongside a p-value.
